I'm just some guy.

I love physics, sure, but I understand none of it. I'm stuck at pop science. Late at night, thoughts about the quantum world, extra-dimensional universes and whatnot start crawling into my head and I can't sleep. Sometimes I get this wild urge to go negative on the Kelvin scale and pull off the impossible. Negative volume and all.

Anyway. This evening I consumed some content on relativity. And every single time I think about it, every time,

The fact that if I increase my velocity, time itself will start moving *slower* for me!!!

Why does this happen? How does it happen? I was talking to Claude about it.

Let's say I'm on a spaceship. I can see my friend back on Earth through the window. If I start moving at a really high speed, I stay young. My friend gets old. Now, if I keep watching my friend through the window, will I get to see a timelapse of him going from a teenager to an old man?

Claude's answer was:
As I keep moving away from my friend, every photon reflected off him takes longer and longer to reach my eyes, because the distance between us keeps growing (think about it… stars that are lightyears away… even further than that… you and I drifting apart, further and further…), so the photons have to cover more and more distance to get to me. Because of this, I will see him in slow-mo, and he won't age that much in my eyes!! On the flip side, if I point the spaceship toward my friend instead, I'll see a timelapse, him aging rapidly before my eyes.

And in both cases, whatever I see when I look at my friend, my friend sees the exact opposite when he looks at me. (Assuming he can aim the telescope right, that is!) So if I'm watching him grow old, he's watching me stay frozen in my teenage years for what feels like forever. And vice versa.

Right, I thought. Twin paradox.

At this point in the conversation, something clicked.

In this graph, if I take my friend as stationary, he traces a path parallel to the x-axis. And me, since I'm moving fast, my slope goes positive.

I couldn't quite figure out which axis held which quantity. But something was definitely forming!!!

If I cut out a specific section of the graph (cut it in a way where I'd have to run the scissors parallel to the y-axis twice), the length of my path in that section comes out longer than my friend's! (And vice versa, if I take myself as stationary and my friend as the moving one, then…)

If I treat the length of that cut-out path as "how much we've aged", does that make any sense? I asked Claude.

The answer came back:
"Yes! You've basically just invented spacetime diagrams (Minkowski diagrams) yourself. That's exactly the right intuition."

It also said my setup was more or less correct. I had made a mistake though, by treating path length as a proxy for age difference, I'd assumed "longer path = more aging." But apparently in spacetime, a straight line corresponds to the *maximum* proper time! That part went a little over my head. I don't really venture much beyond Euclidean geometry.

But I think I'm on the right track. The right intuitions are showing up. *I think.* "I think" being the key phrase here. There's a long way to go!

And somehow, just thinking that makes me really happy.

I read somewhere that people love sharing their joy with others.

I want to share mine with whoever's reading this.

So I'm writing.
